**Lane:** Roguelite horror / psychological horror. Inspired by Buckshot Roulette, Clover Pit, Inscryption, Faith, Mouthwashing — small-footprint, mechanic-driven dread, runs that fit in a sitting.

**Pillar check applied to all entries:**
1. The World Teaches Itself — no tutorials; rules are absorbed through the loop.
2. The Mechanic Is the Meaning — the dread *is* the system, not narration on top.
3. Convention Is a Foundation — uses roguelite literacy as the floor, then twists.
4. Nothing Ships Unfinished — every entry below has a scope floor a solo dev can actually finish.

## 1. Confessional

- **Logline:** Buckshot Roulette in a confessional booth — the priest decides the penance, and the wrong call lets something walk back into the parish.
- **Core Mechanic:** Penitents kneel one at a time. Their confessions are fragmentary, and you assign penance from a small deck of cards (Hail Marys, fasting, pilgrimage, silence). Wrong penance = the sin doesn't stay confessed and ripples into the next confession. Each run is one night.
- **Genre:** Horror, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Buckshot Roulette (single-room intensity), Inscryption (deck-as-judgment), Faith: The Unholy Trinity (Catholic-horror register)
- **Market Notes:** Catholic horror has a durable niche audience (The Mortuary Assistant, Faith). Confessional is iconic and visually unmistakable in a 5-second clip. Risk: religious sensitivity; needs careful tone.
- **Scope Floor:** One booth, 12 confession archetypes that recombine, 8 penance cards, 3 endings tied to how many sins escaped. Pure UI + audio. Voice work for confessions is the production-cost lever.
- **Differentiation:** 5
- **Market Signal:** 4
- **Personal Resonance:** 4
- **Solo Feasibility:** 4

## 2. Pulse

- **Logline:** Papers Please at 4am — you're an emergency dispatcher during a slow-rolling crisis, and your earlier triage decisions become the bodies you hear about later.
- **Core Mechanic:** Calls come in over a single screen UI. You triage (police / fire / medical / hold), with limited dispatch resources and overlapping events. Some calls are different perspectives on the same incident — recognizing the connection is the skill. The night escalates into something the player slowly realizes isn't a normal crisis.
- **Genre:** Horror, Puzzle
- **Reference Games:** Papers Please (judgment under pressure), Buckshot Roulette (one-screen intensity), 911 Operator (genre chassis)
- **Market Notes:** Dispatcher games have proven appeal but no horror-flavored entry. Strong streamability — viewers can hear the calls. Tonally close to Mouthwashing's grounded dread.
- **Scope Floor:** One night, 40 call events, 5 dispatch resource types. No map — calls are listed by address. Voice acting for callers is the cost driver; 80% can be text logs with audio stings.
- **Differentiation:** 5
- **Market Signal:** 4
- **Personal Resonance:** 3
- **Solo Feasibility:** 4

## 3. The Long Drive

- **Logline:** Iron Lung in a car — one driver, one highway, one night, and the radio is the only thing telling you what's outside the headlights.
- **Core Mechanic:** First-person inside a single car. Driving is automatic; player controls are the radio dial, the headlights, the speed, and whether to acknowledge the passenger. Stations carry information — some real, some not. Each run is one drive between two towns. Procedural roadside events.
- **Genre:** Horror, Adventure, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Iron Lung (claustrophobic single-vehicle dread), Pacific Drive (driving as horror loop), Mothered (psychological car horror)
- **Market Notes:** Car-as-horror-space is having a moment (Pacific Drive, MOTHERED). Cheap to produce — minimal exterior environment, all atmosphere is sound and lighting.
- **Scope Floor:** One stretch of highway with 8 procedural events, 6 radio stations, one passenger. ~30-45 minute run. Audio is the load-bearing element.
- **Differentiation:** 4
- **Market Signal:** 5
- **Personal Resonance:** 4
- **Solo Feasibility:** 4

## 4. The Lighthouse Keeper's Shift

- **Logline:** A roguelite about which ships you signal to in the fog — and which ones you let pass without acknowledgment.
- **Core Mechanic:** A lighthouse station with a foghorn, a beam control, a logbook, and a window. Ships pass in the fog, identifiable only by silhouette and sound signature. Some need help (signal them in); others should be ignored (anything you signal becomes aware of you). The shift escalates when you signal wrong.
- **Genre:** Horror, Puzzle, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Buckshot Roulette (single-room reading-the-situation), The Lighthouse (1939) (atmospheric reference), Return of the Obra Dinn (silhouette-based identification)
- **Market Notes:** Lighthouse settings are evergreen in horror but rarely interactive. Strong distinctive aesthetic. Risk: too quiet for streaming if not paced well.
- **Scope Floor:** One lighthouse, 15 ship silhouette + sound combos, escalating fog density. 6 ending states based on what got in. Static art for ships, atmospheric audio.
- **Differentiation:** 5
- **Market Signal:** 3
- **Personal Resonance:** 3
- **Solo Feasibility:** 5

## 5. Static

- **Logline:** Inscryption at a CRT-repair workbench — every TV that comes in plays a problem, and some of the problems aren't electrical.
- **Core Mechanic:** A vintage TV repair shop. Each night, TVs are dropped off. The "problem" plays on the screen — a distorted broadcast, a memory loop, a face. You diagnose using a small toolset (oscilloscope, swap tubes, tune the dial). Some repairs make things worse for whoever owns the TV. Run = one night's queue.
- **Genre:** Horror, Puzzle, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Inscryption (workbench-as-game), Signalis (CRT/analog horror aesthetic), Hypnospace Outlaw (hands-on tech-as-narrative)
- **Market Notes:** Analog horror is enormous on YouTube right now (Local 58, The Mandela Catalogue) but has minimal commercial-game representation. Visual hook is unmistakable in a 5-second clip.
- **Scope Floor:** One workbench, 20 broken-TV scenarios, 5 diagnostic tools. Pixel art / CRT shader carries the aesthetic cheaply. Per-TV "broadcast" is the content-cost driver.
- **Differentiation:** 5
- **Market Signal:** 5
- **Personal Resonance:** 4
- **Solo Feasibility:** 4

## 6. Lucky Strike

- **Logline:** Clover Pit on a bowling lane — you're alone in a closed alley after-hours, and the score sheet is keeping track of more than the game.
- **Core Mechanic:** A single bowling lane. Each frame: choose a pin pattern to set up, throw, score. Pin patterns occasionally spell things. The score sheet has columns that aren't bowling stats. Run = one game (10 frames). Outcomes shift based on what patterns you set and what scores accumulate in the wrong columns.
- **Genre:** Horror, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Clover Pit (single-machine horror loop), Buckshot Roulette (one-room session length), Twin Peaks (bowling-alley liminal aesthetic)
- **Market Notes:** Direct comp to Clover Pit — needs differentiation through the scoring/notation mechanic, not just the setting. Bowling alley as horror space is genuinely fresh.
- **Scope Floor:** One lane, 10 frames, 12 pattern options, a layered score sheet with hidden columns. ~20-minute runs. Low art ceiling — most action is on the lane and on a piece of paper.
- **Differentiation:** 4
- **Market Signal:** 4
- **Personal Resonance:** 3
- **Solo Feasibility:** 5

## 7. The Night Shift Audit

- **Logline:** A roguelite about reconciling a small business's books overnight — and discovering, line by line, what the business is actually doing.
- **Core Mechanic:** A desk, a ledger, a calculator, a phone. Each shift has a pile of receipts, deposit slips, and inventory counts. Reconcile them. Some entries don't add up; some are encoded; some require calling a number on a card to verify. Each run = one night. The truth assembles itself out of the math.
- **Genre:** Horror, Puzzle
- **Reference Games:** Papers Please (paperwork as gameplay), Return of the Obra Dinn (deduction structure), Buckshot Roulette (single-session pacing)
- **Market Notes:** Bureaucratic horror has a cult audience. The accounting framing is fresh — no direct comp. Risk: harder to demo in a 5-second clip than visual-horror titles.
- **Scope Floor:** One office, 5 shifts, 30 reconciliation puzzles, a working in-game calculator. Pure UI. Voice-on-phone is the production cost.
- **Differentiation:** 5
- **Market Signal:** 3
- **Personal Resonance:** 3
- **Solo Feasibility:** 5

## 8. Buzzer

- **Logline:** A 1970s-game-show roguelite where the prizes don't quite match the questions and the audience laughs at the wrong things.
- **Core Mechanic:** You are a contestant. Three rounds. The host asks questions; you buzz in and answer. "Wrong" answers don't trigger the standard fail state — the prize structure shifts. Recognizing what's actually being measured is the metagame. Run = one taping.
- **Genre:** Horror, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Buckshot Roulette (host-and-player tension), Inscryption (game-show-as-mask), The Stanley Parable (subverted format)
- **Market Notes:** Game-show framing is a strong distinctive aesthetic — saturated color, period audio, recognizable iconography. Streamer-friendly. Risk: tone is hard to nail without strong voice work.
- **Scope Floor:** One stage, one host (voiced), 60 questions across 3 rounds, 4 prize-structure outcomes. Performance assets are the cost driver.
- **Differentiation:** 5
- **Market Signal:** 3
- **Personal Resonance:** 3
- **Solo Feasibility:** 3

## 9. The Subway Driver

- **Logline:** One driver, one cab, one final route at 3am — and the line ends at stations that haven't been on the map for years.
- **Core Mechanic:** First-person subway cab. You drive a circuit. Each station prompt: stop / don't stop / pull emergency brake / open doors. Some stations should not exist. Some passengers should not be at the platforms they're at. Run = one circuit (~15 stations).
- **Genre:** Horror, Adventure, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Iron Lung (single-vehicle horror), MiSide (transit horror), Liminal-space horror genre at large
- **Market Notes:** Liminal space horror is huge on TikTok. Subway/transit aesthetic is universally recognizable. Cheap-to-produce environment (one cab interior + tunnel sequences).
- **Scope Floor:** One train cab, 8 station archetypes + 4 anomalous stations, ~30-minute run. Tunnel transitions are atmospheric/audio rather than detailed environments.
- **Differentiation:** 4
- **Market Signal:** 5
- **Personal Resonance:** 3
- **Solo Feasibility:** 4

## 10. Pawn

- **Logline:** Strange Horticulture meets Buckshot Roulette — a pawnshop counter at night where the wrong appraisal lets the wrong thing into your shop.
- **Core Mechanic:** Customers arrive with items. You appraise (offer / refuse / counter). Some items have provenance you can read; some items ARE provenance — they carry something with them. Buying the wrong thing changes the shop overnight. Run = one shift.
- **Genre:** Horror, Puzzle, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Strange Horticulture (item-taxonomy mechanic), Buckshot Roulette (single-counter pacing), Cultist Simulator (occult-object handling)
- **Market Notes:** Strange Horticulture's success (300k+ Steam reviews) shows item-handling-as-puzzle works commercially. Pawnshop framing is fresh. Strong streamable hook (each item is content).
- **Scope Floor:** One counter, 50 items with property tags, 8 customers per shift, 4 shifts. Static art per item. The shop-state-changing system is the engineering challenge.
- **Differentiation:** 4
- **Market Signal:** 5
- **Personal Resonance:** 4
- **Solo Feasibility:** 4

## 11. Vigil

- **Logline:** A hospice night-nurse roguelite where the small comforts you choose to give become the only thing standing between certain patients and what's coming for them.
- **Core Mechanic:** A four-room hospice ward. Each shift you make rounds: read charts, administer meds, sit with patients, call family. Time is limited; not every patient gets equal attention. Some patients are dying tonight. Some aren't but should be. Some... aren't dying at all. Run = one shift.
- **Genre:** Horror, Sim
- **Reference Games:** Spiritfarer (death-positive emotional register), Buckshot Roulette (run-length pacing), The Mortuary Assistant (medical-horror genre)
- **Market Notes:** Emotional + horror combination has a passionate audience but is harder to market. Risk: tonally delicate; could read as exploitative if not handled with care. Personal resonance ceiling is high.
- **Scope Floor:** 4 patients, 6 round-actions, 3 shifts. Voice work for patients is the production-cost lever. Soft, painterly art style keeps production costs low and serves the tone.
- **Differentiation:** 5
- **Market Signal:** 3
- **Personal Resonance:** 5
- **Solo Feasibility:** 3

## 12. Conductor

- **Logline:** A roguelite about conducting a small orchestra in an empty hall, where the music itself is wrong in different ways every night and your job is to lead the players through it.
- **Core Mechanic:** A podium with a baton. Inputs: tempo, dynamics, cue specific sections. The score plays — but the score has small wrongnesses (a bar that shouldn't be there, a section that goes silent, a player who keeps drifting). You decide whether to enforce the original score or let the music become what it's becoming. Run = one concert (4 pieces).
- **Genre:** Horror, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Hi-Fi Rush (rhythm + agency), Inscryption (act-by-act revelation structure), Beethoven's "Hammerklavier" reference for musical-horror tone
- **Market Notes:** No direct comp for conducting-as-horror-mechanic. Risk: rhythm/music games are technically demanding; sound design is load-bearing. Differentiation extremely high if execution lands.
- **Scope Floor:** 4 pieces, 6 sections each, a baton input system, a "wrongness" notation layer. Music composition is the dominant cost — needs a composer collaborator.
- **Differentiation:** 5
- **Market Signal:** 2
- **Personal Resonance:** 3
- **Solo Feasibility:** 2

## 13. The Reading

- **Logline:** Buckshot Roulette at a fortune teller's table — every card you draw becomes what happens to the customer when they walk out.
- **Core Mechanic:** A small table at a carnival or back-room. Customers sit. You shuffle, draw, interpret. The interpretation is the input — what you say (selected from a small menu) becomes what manifests. Some readings should be softened; some should be told straight. The deck is finite per night and the cards remember what you've said.
- **Genre:** Horror, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** The Cosmic Wheel Sisterhood (tarot-as-narrative), Buckshot Roulette (table-and-stranger format), Inscryption (deck-driven session structure)
- **Market Notes:** Tarot games sell well in this audience (Cosmic Wheel charted). Horror inflection is fresh. Strong streamable framing — readings have inherent narrative arc.
- **Scope Floor:** 78-card deck, 6 customer archetypes, 5 nights, branching outcome tree. Card art is the production-cost lever; can be procedurally generated or commissioned in batches.
- **Differentiation:** 4
- **Market Signal:** 4
- **Personal Resonance:** 4
- **Solo Feasibility:** 4

## 14. The Cellar Door

- **Logline:** A sommelier roguelite where the wrong bottle pulled releases something the cellar was built to hold down.
- **Core Mechanic:** A small wine cellar with a ladder UI. Customers (or your own employer) request bottles by description. You read labels — vintage, region, terroir — and pull. Some bottles are mislabeled. Some are not bottles. Pulling a wrong one cracks the seal on something. Run = one evening of service.
- **Genre:** Horror, Puzzle, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Strange Horticulture (taxonomy mechanic), Buckshot Roulette (escalating-stakes format), Cultist Simulator (occult-object handling)
- **Market Notes:** Wine cellar setting is fresh and visually distinctive. Niche but durable horror audience. Lower differentiation than Pawn — partial overlap.
- **Scope Floor:** 60 bottles with label properties, 12 customer requests per night, 4 nights. Pure UI. Heavy on text design.
- **Differentiation:** 3
- **Market Signal:** 3
- **Personal Resonance:** 2
- **Solo Feasibility:** 5

## 15. Final Cut

- **Logline:** A roguelite about being the projectionist at a small theater where the reels keep arriving from films that don't exist.
- **Core Mechanic:** A projection booth. Each night, reels arrive in cans. You inspect, splice, thread, run. Reels are out of order; some are from a different film entirely; some are from films that never existed. The audience watches in silence. Decisions: which reel to load next, when to cut, whether to stop the show. Run = one screening.
- **Genre:** Horror, Puzzle, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Inscryption (analog-medium horror), Pony Island (medium-as-horror), Skinamarink (vintage-celluloid aesthetic)
- **Market Notes:** Projection-booth aesthetic is iconic and underused. Lo-fi celluloid look = strong art-direction-on-a-budget. Tonally adjacent to analog horror trend.
- **Scope Floor:** 30 reels, 6 screenings, splice/thread minigame. Film snippets are the content-cost driver — can be procedurally distorted footage.
- **Differentiation:** 5
- **Market Signal:** 4
- **Personal Resonance:** 4
- **Solo Feasibility:** 4

## 16. The Kiln

- **Logline:** A crematorium night-attendant roguelite where the bodies keep arriving without paperwork and some of them shouldn't be here.
- **Core Mechanic:** A processing room. Bodies arrive on gurneys with manifests. You check paperwork, prep, load the kiln. Some manifests are wrong; some are missing; some bodies should not be cremated. Decisions: process / hold / call the coroner / send back. Run = one shift.
- **Genre:** Horror, Sim, Puzzle
- **Reference Games:** The Mortuary Assistant (mortician-horror), Papers Please (document-handling), Buckshot Roulette (shift-length intensity)
- **Market Notes:** Mortuary Assistant is one of the highest-rated horror titles of recent years (95% on Steam). Direct genre adjacency. Risk: tonal sensitivity required.
- **Scope Floor:** 20 manifest archetypes, 8 bodies per shift, 4 shifts, 3 endings. Static body sprites + paperwork UI. Audio carries 80% of the dread.
- **Differentiation:** 4
- **Market Signal:** 5
- **Personal Resonance:** 3
- **Solo Feasibility:** 5

## 17. Standing Wave

- **Logline:** Iron Lung as a shortwave radio operator on a remote island — the stations cycle, and one of them has started responding to you specifically.
- **Core Mechanic:** A radio rig in a one-room shack. Tune the dial through the band, log what you hear, decide whether to broadcast a response. Some stations carry numbers, some carry voices, some carry nothing — but the nothing has a pattern. Run = one night of logging.
- **Genre:** Horror, Adventure, Roguelike
- **Reference Games:** Iron Lung (single-station claustrophobic horror), Signalis (radio-as-horror aesthetic), Numbers stations / The Conet Project (real-world reference)
- **Market Notes:** Numbers-station horror has a built-in audience (analog horror, conspiracy-curious). Aesthetic is cheap to produce — one room, one rig. Audio is the load-bearing element.
- **Scope Floor:** One shack, 15 station archetypes, 6 nights, a logging UI. Audio assets dominate the production cost — pre-recorded transmissions and procedural distortion.
- **Differentiation:** 4
- **Market Signal:** 4
- **Personal Resonance:** 4
- **Solo Feasibility:** 5

## Conflicts / things to flag

- **Comp risk:** Lucky Strike sits closest to Clover Pit. Differentiation must be explicit — the layered score sheet/notation has to be visibly more than Clover Pit's slot mechanic, or this concept gets read as a clone.
- **Existing-DB tension:** The current Notion DB is dominated by slow-comprehension puzzle/horror entries (Archivist, The Handler, Last Call, Sorting Office, etc.) that pre-date the genre lane decision. None are roguelites in the Buckshot Roulette mold. They aren't *wrong* — they fit the older Outer-Wilds-leaning manifesto — but a triage pass against the new manifesto is worth scheduling. Some will still pass; some may want to be re-tagged or archived.
