# Practical Process for Finding and Selecting Indie Game Ideas

Based on themes commonly discussed in [GameDiscoverCo](https://newsletter.gamediscover.co?utm_source=chatgpt.com) and indie developer postmortems/discussions on Reddit, the strongest recurring idea is:

> Do not start from “What game do I want to make?”
> 
> Start from “What game can I realistically finish that already has visible player demand and room to stand out?” ([newsletter.gamediscover.co](https://newsletter.gamediscover.co/p/your-complete-game-discovery-primer?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Your complete 'game discovery' primer - by Simon Carless"))

---

# 1. Build an “Opportunity List”

Create a spreadsheet with 20–50 candidate ideas.

For each idea, write:

| Field                      | Example                            |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Core fantasy               | “Run a haunted convenience store”  |
| Genre                      | Roguelite deckbuilder              |
| Audience                   | Cozy/horror stream viewers         |
| Comparable games           | _Buckshot Roulette_, _Inscryption_ |
| Why players buy it         | Tension + replayability            |
| Why it could spread online | Funny deaths, streamer reactions   |
| Scope estimate             | 6–12 months solo                   |
| Your unfair advantage      | Strong atmosphere/audio            |

Do **not** evaluate ideas yet.

Goal: volume first.

---

# 2. Mine Existing Demand Instead of Inventing Blindly

Look for games that already prove players want the experience.

Use:

- Steam tag pages
    
- “Popular Upcoming”
    
- Steam festivals
    
- YouTube creators
    
- TikTok clips
    
- Reddit communities
    
- Steam reviews of successful indie games
    

Look for:

- Genres with recurring hits
    
- Games players want “more of”
    
- Complaints in reviews
    
- Underserved combinations
    

Examples:

|Existing Demand|Possible Angle|
|---|---|
|Vampire Survivors|Different fantasy/theme|
|Lethal Company|Singleplayer equivalent|
|Papers, Please|New setting/profession|
|Buckshot Roulette|Expandable social systems|

Do not chase originality.  
Chase **familiar + distinct**.

GameDiscoverCo repeatedly emphasizes discoverability and recognizable hooks. ([newsletter.gamediscover.co](https://newsletter.gamediscover.co/p/your-complete-game-discovery-primer?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Your complete 'game discovery' primer - by Simon Carless"))

# 3. Filter for Solo-Dev Feasibility

Remove ideas that require:

- Large content pipelines
    
- MMO/network complexity
    
- Massive animation workloads
    
- Large narrative production
    
- Open worlds
    
- AAA polish expectations
    

Prefer ideas with:

- Systemic replayability
    
- Emergent gameplay
    
- Short session loops
    
- Reusable environments
    
- Simple art styles
    
- Strong mechanics over content quantity
    

A useful rule:

|Green Flag|Red Flag|
|---|---|
|Deep systems|Huge asset counts|
|Replayability|Handcrafted hours|
|One core mechanic|Many disconnected mechanics|
|Strong hook|Generic execution|

---

# 4. Evaluate “Marketability Before Development”

Before building the game, answer:

## The Thumbnail Test

Would someone click this instantly?

Examples:

- “Russian roulette with a shotgun”
    
- “Manage a cult in a grocery store”
    
- “Cozy game but the villagers are hiding a monster”
    

If the premise requires long explanation, it is weaker.

---

## The GIF Test

Can you show the fun in 5 seconds?

Strong indie concepts generate:

- Instant visual understanding
    
- Chaos
    
- Surprise
    
- Tension
    
- Comedy
    
- Skill expression
    

---

## The Streamer Test

Would a creator naturally produce reactions or stories?

Games spread when players generate content for you.

---

# 5. Score Every Idea

Use a weighted scoring system.

|Category|Score (1–5)|
|---|---|
|Market demand exists||
|Easy to explain||
|Strong visual hook||
|Replayability||
|Solo-dev feasible||
|Can finish in <1 year||
|Personal motivation||
|Streamer/social potential||
|Low asset burden||
|Strong differentiation||

Remove anything below your threshold.

---

# 6. Prototype Fast

Do not fully commit yet.

Build a prototype in:

- 3 days
    
- 1 week
    
- or 1 month max
    

Prototype only the:

- core interaction
    
- emotional hook
    
- gameplay loop
    

Do **not** build:

- menus
    
- progression
    
- story
    
- polish
    

Question:

> “Is the core loop still fun after 20 minutes?”

If not:

- pivot
    
- simplify
    
- or kill it quickly
    

Pre-release experimentation and rapid validation are repeatedly emphasized in indie dev research and discussions. ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.17183?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Pre-Release Experimentation in Indie Game Development: An Interview Survey"))

# 7. Test Audience Interest Before Full Production

Before serious development:

## Make:

- Steam capsule mockups
    
- GIFs
    
- short clips
    
- fake screenshots
    
- short trailer concepts
    

## Post them:

- Reddit
    
- Twitter/X
    
- TikTok
    
- Discords
    
- genre communities
    

Measure:

- comments
    
- shares
    
- wishlists
    
- clickthrough
    
- retention
    

You are testing:

- the fantasy
    
- the hook
    
- the presentation
    

—not polish.

---

# 8. Prefer “Small but Sharp”

Most successful solo indies are:

- narrow
    
- focused
    
- emotionally clear
    
- mechanically deep
    

Bad scope:

- “Open world survival RPG”
    

Good scope:

- “One mechanic explored deeply”
    

---

# 9. Choose Games With Compounding Content

Best solo-friendly structures:

|Structure|Why It Works|
|---|---|
|Roguelites|Replayability from systems|
|Extraction loops|Emergent stories|
|Simulation|Player-generated variety|
|Sandbox systems|Content multiplies itself|
|Physics chaos|Social media moments|
|Deckbuilders|Low asset requirements|

Weak solo structures:

- cinematic RPGs
    
- heavily scripted campaigns
    
- content-heavy MMOs
    

---

# 10. Final Greenlight Checklist

Do not start full production unless most are true:

- Clear audience exists
    
- Comparable games succeed
    
- Your angle is immediately understandable
    
- Scope is survivable solo
    
- Prototype is genuinely fun
    
- Marketing clips already work
    
- You can explain the game in one sentence
    
- The game creates stories/reactions
    
- You still want to work on it after prototyping
    

If several are false:

- simplify
    
- reposition
    
- or abandon early

# A Practical Solo-Dev Formula

A reliable formula for modern indie ideas:

```text
Proven genre
+ fresh thematic twist
+ strong social/streaming moments
+ manageable scope
+ replayable systems
= high-potential solo project
```

Examples:

```text
Papers, Please + paranormal investigation
Balatro + fishing
Lethal Company + singleplayer horror
Factorio + necromancy
```

# Recommended Weekly Routine

|Day|Task|
|---|---|
|Monday|Research Steam/recent indie hits|
|Tuesday|Add 5 new ideas|
|Wednesday|Prototype one mechanic|
|Thursday|Make mock marketing assets|
|Friday|Post clips/screenshots|
|Saturday|Analyze reactions|
|Sunday|Kill weak ideas aggressively|

# Key Principle

The safest indie strategy is usually:

```text
Do not invent a market.
Enter an existing market with a sharper hook and smaller scope.
```

That pattern appears repeatedly across indie success stories, discoverability analysis, and developer postmortems.
