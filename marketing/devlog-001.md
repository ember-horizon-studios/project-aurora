I started Ember Horizon Studios because I wanted more autonomy in my life. I felt like I was on a treadmill, with the course of my life set for me, and I wanted to grab the steering wheel and explore a different path. My hope is that my lifelong love for gaming, amplified by the act of creation, will provide a deeper sense of purpose and a way to give back to the community.

I want to be making games that make people think. Not just for the dopamine hits, but for the intrigue and the philosophy that lingers after the screen goes dark. Horror and psychological horror are effective vehicles for that — they lower your guard before they make you confront something real. But I don't want to be boxed in by "scary." I want to make games that stay with you, and horror just happens to be one of the most honest ways I know how to do that.

I'm also inherently analytical. I can't help it. So Ember Horizon games won't be made on instinct alone — every concept gets pressure-tested from market research to mechanic design before a single line of code gets written. Not because I'm afraid of risk, but because I think the best games are the ones where every decision was made on purpose. I'd rather spend a week validating an idea than six months building the wrong one. That being said, I am hoping to also let loose a little during development

Right now, the studio is standing up from scratch. The legal and financial foundation is in place — LLC registered, developer accounts open, toolchain running. The public-facing side launched quietly last week. What comes next is the part that actually matters: choosing the right debut concept and building it the right way.

I'm triage-testing a handful of game ideas right now. Each one gets evaluated against a set of criteria — differentiation, market signal, personal resonance, solo feasibility. The one that survives becomes the debut title. I'll share more on the shortlist soon.

This is where it starts.
