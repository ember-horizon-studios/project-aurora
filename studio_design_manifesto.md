## Core Vision

This section defines the studio’s reason for existence and the emotional impact it aims to have on players.

- **Mission Statement:** Where technical proficiency meets conceptual depth. I design games as a medium for personal expression, challenging the boundaries of how emotion is felt through play.
- **Design Pillars:** 3–5 non-negotiable principles that must be present in every game (e.g., "Player Agency," "Tactile Feedback," or "Emergent Narrative").

## Gameplay Philosophy

This defines the studio’s approach to the fundamental building blocks of a game.

- **The "Fun" Mandate:** Defining what the studio considers engaging. Is it the "30 seconds of fun" loop, or is it deep, systemic complexity?
- **Approach to Difficulty:** Will the studio prioritize "accessibility for all" or "mastery through hardship"?
- **Innovation vs. Convention:** A stance on when to use established genre tropes and when to intentionally break them to innovate.

---

## Areas of Design

Identify the specific domains where the studio will concentrate its expertise.

- **Systems Design:** Rules for how game elements interact (e.g., "All systems must be interconnected so players can find creative solutions").
- **Narrative Design:** How story is told—is it through cutscenes, or is it "environmental storytelling" where the world tells the tale?
- **Level/World Design:** The philosophy behind space—linear "rollercoaster" levels vs. open-ended "playgrounds."
- **UX/UI & Accessibility:** Standards for how the player interfaces with the game. A manifesto might state: "The HUD should never break immersion" or "No game leaves our doors without Colorblind Mode."

---

## Technical & Production Standards

How the studio actually builds the vision.

- **Prototyping First:** A commitment to "finding the fun" in gray-box environments before committing to high-fidelity art.
- **"Brutal" Scoping:** A philosophy on cutting features that don't support the core pillars to ensure polish over quantity.
- **Pipeline Integrity:** Establishing that tools and workflows are as important as the game itself (e.g., "If a designer can't tweak a value without an engineer, the tool is broken").

---

## Culture and Collaboration

The "human" side of design.

- **Interdisciplinary Respect:** Encouraging artists, coders, and writers to speak the same "design language."
- **The Playtest Culture:** A mandate that everyone—from the CEO to the intern—must play the game weekly and provide feedback.
- **Failure as a Feature:** Explicitly stating that "failed" prototypes are valuable data points, not wasted time.
